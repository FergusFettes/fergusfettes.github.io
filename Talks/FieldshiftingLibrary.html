It is not known whether the total library is really infinite or not. In the millenia since it was discovered we have worked tirelessly to map and organize it, but we have not yet reached any kind of limit or boundary, nor have we found a single repeated work.

Let heaven exist, though my place be in hell. Let me be outraged and annihilated, but for one instant, in one being, let Your enormous Library be justified.

One of the early poets of the library wrote these words. I am happy to announce that since that time we have made great progress on the project of justifying the library.

We faithfully organize and categorize all that we find. And indeed, over the millenia, the catagolgues have grown to start to challenge the immensity.

Library of Unwritten Papers












1

This is the Library of Unwritten Papers. It contains near-Earth works, works that might, but for some small contingency (or some gross inconveninece) have been written in this world. Perhaps someone got hit by a bus or a meteor on the way to the publishing house. Perhaps they just had a bad hangover. Whatever the reason, here we collect works that very nearly existed.

From now on, which I say ‘the library’, it is the library of unwritten papers that I am talking about.

Works in the Library: Background

The number of papers written every year continues to grow, but is currently somewhere in the region of 3 million.

The number of books is similar, so there are around 6-8 million significant works added to your libraries every year.

  • 2-3 million technical papers p.a.
  • growing by 4-9% per year
  • total number of books similar

Likewise, the number of papers not written each year grows– but several orders of magnitude faster. By some estimates there are over a billion works unwritten every year.

  • 2-4 oom more unpublished works
  • estimated at >1 billion p.a.

  • we group works by similarity to avoid too much repetition

Now, before you suggest that these unwritten works are just minor variations on when we have– we group works by similarity to avoid overcounting.

  • exactly where we set this cutoff is a matter of great debate

Lets say you have 10 works. If you require 100% similarity to group them, you have 10 works. If you require 0% similarity, you have one work. In between you have cutoffs at different phonon lines. At 80% you would have 6 works. At 55%, two.

Exactly where we set this cutoff is a matter of great debate. With our current cutoffs we get the following estimates:

  • research: each researcher doesn’t publish about 100 papers per year
  • books: the number of unpublished books annually is on the order of the human population
  • poetry: estimates say 90% of the human population fails to publish a poem every year
  • poetry: some outliers don’t write up to hundreds of potential poems a day

To appreciate these numbers it helps to understand the structure of cultural phylogeny. Regular phylogeny has this branching pattern, where entities get further and further from one another over time, which makes it harder for them to hybridize.

Cultural phylogeny on the other hand has this reticulated, self-intersecting, rhizomatic structure. This causes a combinatorial explosion in possible works– even taking, as we are, only unique, original works that are very likely to have been produced.

Withdrawals

Now, you may be wondering, how does one go about finding a paper like the Cyanide paper in the library, how do you put in a request for it, how do you withdraw it.

Of course you could do like the early librarians did– just read through endless papers until you stumble upon a good one. But time is fleeting.

You could try doing a keyword search, limiting your papers to certian domains. This is a slight improvement, but in the end you are still left reading through countless articles trying to find the good ones. The problem is we don’t have citation counts or peer reviews. The papers haven’t been published, so how could they be cited? You can still try adding ‘high citation count’ to your query, and it does work. But we can do better.

The thing is, adding keywords and other filters to your search, it functions as if you are adding information to the document you want to see. And this is the right approach. The more information you put in, the better your results will be. You are trying to weave a specific document from the probability manifold with only your intention as a guide.

Similarity search: why does this work?



1

Why does this work? One group of scholars who work on these techniques usually perform their searching using papers in neurology, and target the developmental biology department. They argue that functional invariants between these disciplines as well as a deep symmetry in both hardware and physiology allows for this sort of translation.

It might be something like the following: if the original paper faithfully reproduces some structure that is present in the material under observation– in this case in the collective behaviour of neurons– and if, as we suppose, the world is full of symmetries and harmonies, echos and shadows, if these kinds of reflections and translations are an essential part of the material of the world, then the deep structure that the original paper identified should be present also in other displines and other areas.

Well, this may be true, I hear you object, when shifting between two neighboring disciplines. After all, neurology and cellular morphology fundamentally deal with cells, the work is done with microscopes and the apparatus of science.

Universal Knowledge

So, to summarise. In exploring the library of unwritten papers, we have found an interesting technique for finding good works. This method works by exploiting deep symmetry between works.

Universal Knowledge

The beauty of the technique is, if you do this successfully, you have not only found the new paper, you have found the symmetry itself. One paper may contain many such symmetries, and a successful translation is a hint at a symmetry.

So dear listener, I hope I have allayed any skepticism you might have had, and I hope I have inspired you to approach this with curiosity and ask– which of my favourite works might also make interesting statements in other disciplines that I am fond of or interested in? In this way, not only do you get to read your favourite papers again with fresh eyes in a new domain, and to learn about a new domain through a familiar lense. You also get a glimpse of a deep symmetry in knowledge.

And perhaps, when you do this, you can try to understand what it is exactly about that particular piece of research that makes it work in this way. What is the deep resonant structure that is forming the bridge. How does this paper, this very particular piece of research on this very particular topic, reflect something deep and abstract that might be true of any discipline that you turn your attention to?
