How the Origin of Life Reveals the Deep Logic of All Complex Systems

Many of the biggest mysteries around us share the same underlying question: how does something radically novel emerge and sustain itself—from nothing more than the chaotic mix of simpler elements? The origin of life on Earth is perhaps the purest example of this fascinating puzzle. How did chemistry alone cross the threshold from mere molecules jostling randomly, into organized structures able to grow, replicate, and evolve? Answering this isn’t just fundamental to biology. It could illuminate something universally profound about how complexity arises everywhere: from simple chemical reactions, to ecosystems and societies, to our economy and the technologies it produces.

It’s appealing to suspect life is not a singular special case—that its emergence might be governed by rules or principles common to complex systems in general. If we managed to clearly define these principles, perhaps the logic by which life emerged four billion years ago could also apply to the emergence of intelligence, civilizations, and even new forms of technology or social organization today.

To explore this intriguing possibility, we need to first strip away life’s complicated surface—the vast machinery of modern cells, their genes, proteins, organelles—and look deeper, down to its simplest features. One particularly compelling answer to the riddle of life’s emergence has recently come from the field of non-equilibrium thermodynamics. This field studies how energy flows through physical systems, and how certain structures can spontaneously form and persist through the continuous dissipation of energy. It’s known as dissipative adaptation, developed recently by MIT physicist Jeremy England.

In essence, dissipative adaptation says something simple and elegant: given the right environmental conditions, matter naturally arranges itself into configurations that efficiently capture energy from available sources and dissipate it into their surroundings as heat. Right away we can see the relevance to life’s origins—life as we know it consumes energy-rich molecules or captures energy from sunlight, maintains itself structurally, grows, and becomes more efficient at further energy capture and dissipation. This insight helps shift our thinking away from imagining life as a miraculous anomaly, and instead implies it’s a structured response to steady inputs of energy and collisions of simple building-block molecules.

We can formalize this picture a bit more precisely. Imagine some collection of matter in contact with a steady energy source—a warm geothermal vent, sunlight streaming on a primordial beach, a chemical redox gradient. Its immediate future will depend on a surprisingly simple set of factors:

  • ΔE (Energy gradient): How accessible and maintainable energy flow is in the environment.
  • T (Temperature stability): The environment needs temperatures moderate enough (a “Goldilocks range”) to let complex arrangements form without being immediately destroyed.
  • Z (Chemical diversity): The presence of varied chemical elements and molecules to form the building blocks of more complex structures.
  • ρ (Density): The density of interactions among these molecules, enabling continuous structural reshuffling and experimentation.

We combine these four key factors into a single, comprehensive measure that captures the environment’s potential for sustaining and evolving complex structures:

[ G_{env} = () S(T) (Z) ]

where: - ( ) measures the available energy gradient per unit of mass—essentially how much energy the system can access. - ( S(T) ) is a function describing how stable molecular structures are in relation to the environmental temperature, sharply declining as temperatures become too high and more gradually declining as temperatures become too cold. - ( (Z) ) is a term quantifying chemical diversity, accounting for the presence of varied building blocks.

This number, (G_{env}), is an environmental upper bound—a measure of how conducive the surroundings are for simple matter to spontaneously self-assemble into organized, complexity-building dissipative structures. High values mean it’s very likely configurations will form spontaneously that efficiently manage these energy flows, continuously experimenting and occasionally stumbling onto configurations that reproduce effectively, or “stick” better in the environment.

But the complexity that emerges isn’t just determined by isolated environmental potential. It also depends historically on what’s come before. This is the idea of accumulated complexity, which we call:

[ G_{cum}(t) = _{0}^{t} G()d ]

Where (G()) represents what fraction of the environmental potential is actually accessed at time (), depending on how organized and sophisticated the previously evolved structure already is.

Initially, if you’re starting simply from elemental chemistry, the fraction of (G_{env}) that can actually be accessed will be very small, because there’s no sophisticated structure yet in place to efficiently harvest most available energy. Over time, though, this fraction increases: complexity emerges as structures build on structures, energy-transducing pathways and storage methods evolve, and chemistry moves from simple molecules to complex autocatalytic cycles, primitive cells, ecosystems, and beyond. At each level, previously accumulated complexity ((G_{cum})) unlocks access to more of the environmental potential ((G_{env})).

This simple but powerful dynamic helps explain why we see complexity arise continuously but unevenly through history. It suggests an accelerating nature to how complex systems evolve: previous complexity constantly unlocks new capability. It also helps explain why complexity can expand surprisingly quickly when it finds itself in new, unexplored niches possessing high environmental potential that are just starting to become accessible—like land when plants first colonized it, or the internet when early computers first came online.

This is only a single step toward a broader goal—a way to unify theories of complexity, energy rate density, thermodynamics, and evolutionary dynamics into one grounded, intuitive framework.

Such a unified theory is ambitious, of course. But consider the power of what it might enable. Rather than viewing research on the origins of life and the evolution of society as separate fields, we could view them as part of a greater exploration into how complexity emerges, sustains, and grows at all scales. Rather than treating biology’s rise from chemistry as detached from the advent of technology and civilization, one theory built on first principles could help us understand both the primordial slime and sophisticated machine intelligence as points along the same continuum.

Suggestively, this continuous viewpoint may even clarify how to drive complexity to beneficial new heights in our own societies—well beyond what we’ve achieved intuitively so far. Then we’d be outlining not just a scientific breakthrough, but perhaps pointing to an entirely new way of understanding and navigating the turbulent waters of the future.

Given all this, let’s begin simply. Dissipative adaptation gives us our first clear insight: complexity’s emergence might indeed be far simpler and more universal than we’ve allowed ourselves to imagine.
