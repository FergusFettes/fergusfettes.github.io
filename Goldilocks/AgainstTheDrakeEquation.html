I made the mistake of looking too closely at the Drake Equation and now I cant unsee how silly it is. Its full of statements like this: “the average number of planets that can potentially support life per star that has planets”. What?

By contrast, here is a sentence from Hansons ‘Grabby Aliens’ model: ‘“Grabby” aliens is our especially simple model of loud aliens, a model with only 3 free parameters, each of which we can estimate to within a factor of 4 from existing data. That standard hard steps model implies a power law (t/k)n appearance function, with two free parameters k and n, and the last parameter is the expansion speed s.’

Bold. Assertive. Thats how you cut through uncertainty, not hand-waving and hedging. Hanson wins this one, hands down.

But can we do better?

The Goldilocks Potential

Hanson still has this great filter/ hard steps view of evolution: “Power n from the history of major events in the evolution of life on Earth”. What is the actual claim here? This doesn’t feel like much of an improvement on Drakes ‘potentially support life’ or ‘fraction of planets that could support life that actually develop life at some point’ or ‘fraction of planets with life that go on to develop intelligent life’. This is now how anything works. Life is not a discrete series of states of matter that occur or do not occur in a stepwise, binary manner.

Life is, clearly, a process. A planet doesn’t simply ‘get life’ at some point like an intergalactic STI. Rather, a region of space has a capacity to support complexity—a “liveliness potential”—based on its local environmental conditions. Let’s call this the Goldilocks potential (G_base), an intensive property determined by local physics and chemistry:

G_base = f(local environmental conditions)

As matter moves through regions of varying G_base over time, complexity can accumulate where conditions are favorable. This accumulated complexity (G_cum) represents the historical integration of G_base along that matter’s trajectory:

G_cum = ∫G_base(t) dt

and we suppose that the ‘amount of life’ or ‘degree of intelligence’ or simply the complexity in a given region of spacetime is some function of these two measures:

G_effective = G_base + h(G_cum)

We can probably even measure it. We could try to use Chaissons proposal and ask, ‘what is the energy rate density of the most energy intensive system on the planet?’ Or if we are unhappy with the idea of drawing arbitrary boundaries we could just slice any region of space up into meter-cubed chunks and measure the ERD for each of them.

Or we could use Assembly Theory. We could divide the planet up into a series of Assembly Spaces, try to find the most fine-grained one or the most universal one for the planet, and calculate its Assembly Index.

Isn’t it a bit presumptive to assume that, given some set of conditions, complexity will start accumulating? And what are those conditions anyway? How do we measure G_base?

Cleary we need an energy source, something to drive the system, some kind of gradient: ΔE. This could be a thermal gradient from a heat source like a star or a hydrothermal vent, or a chemical potential or something more exotic. It also has to be possible for structures to form and to maintain themselves over time– the temperature can’t be too low for changes to occur or too high for them to stick: 0 < T < T_max.

And finally you need a substrate. Defining the substrate is complicated and it can probably take on many different forms. So lets start with the most obvious substrate: the substrate of life as we know it. We should be able to extend the idea to arbitrary substrates in the same way that Assembly Theory does, simply by breaking a system under observation into modular recombinable parts. But if we are thinking about life as we know it, it seems the substrate is, at the most basic, some set of elemental chemcials at some sufficient density. Lets use Z to describe this chemical composition of the environment, and ρ to describe the density.

The relationship is multiplicative - all factors must be non-zero for the process of life to proceed:

G_base = ΔE × S(T) × f(Z) × g(ρ)

  • S(T) is the temperature stability function
  • f(Z) represents chemical diversity potential
  • g(ρ) captures interaction frequency effects

This formula matches well with the requirements of Jeremy Englands model of dissipative adaptation. The model describes how, given the set of circumstances described above, structures will emerge that have the property of dissipating energy. There are a few ways of interpreting dissipative adaptation. One is to say simply that due to the nature of the environment– and particularly the chemical makeup– it is ‘easier’ in a thermodynamic sense to translate some of the energy flux into structure than to simply wait for it to pass on on its own.

And if the energy flux is stable over long timescales, such as near a star, and the temperature and chemical conditions are steady, these stored structures will accumulate over time.

Accumulated Complexity: G_cum

While G_base describes the instantaneous potential for complexity in an environment, G_cum captures something different: the actual complexity that has accumulated over time along a particular trajectory through spacetime.

G_cum represents history made manifest—the structures, patterns, and information-processing capabilities that have emerged and persisted as matter has moved through regions of varying G_base.

Crucially, G_cum exhibits fundamentally non-linear growth. When a region with existing complexity (G_cum > 0) experiences favorable conditions (G_base > 0), the rate of further complexity accumulation isn’t merely additive but multiplicative:

dG_cum/dt = G_base × f(G_cum)

Where f(G_cum) is an increasing function of existing complexity. This captures a profound truth: complexity begets more complexity at an accelerating rate.

The Mechanism of Accumulation

How does this acceleration happen? The answer lies in the process of selection. In environments with persistent energy flows, structures that better capture and dissipate that energy are statistically favored to emerge and persist—exactly as Jeremy England’s dissipative adaptation framework predicts.

This selection process operates at every scale. Initially, selection might favor simple autocatalytic chemical cycles that efficiently dissipate solar energy or chemical gradients. But once these structures exist, they themselves create new selection pressures and new chemical environments.

The substrate for complexity evolves. What begins as selection acting on simple molecules eventually becomes selection acting on complex molecular assemblies, then primitive cells, then organisms, and eventually ecosystems and societies. Each layer of complexity creates the conditions for the next.

Assembly Theory offers a way to quantify this accumulated complexity through the Assembly Index (AI)—the minimum number of steps required to construct a given structure from simpler building blocks. Recent work shows that under selection pressure, structures with much higher Assembly Indices emerge than would form spontaneously. This is precisely what we observe in G_cum accumulation—selection driving the formation of increasingly complex structures.

Measuring G_cum

We can quantify G_cum in a region by examining the distribution of complexity within it. Using Assembly Theory’s approach, this could take the form:

G_cum = ∑[AI(i) × log(N(i))]

Where: - AI(i) is the Assembly Index of structure type i - N(i) is the number of copies of that structure in the region - The sum is taken over all distinct structure types

This formulation captures both the complexity of individual structures and their abundance, providing a measure of the total accumulated complexity in a system.

The Feedback Between G_base and G_cum

A fascinating consequence of accumulated complexity is that it can modify the effective G_base of an environment. Consider Earth’s atmosphere: the oxygen we breathe wasn’t present in the early Earth but emerged as a byproduct of photosynthetic life. This dramatically changed the local G_base by creating new chemical potentials and energy sources.

This suggests a deeper relationship between our measures:

G_effective = G_base + h(G_cum)

Where h(G_cum) represents the enhancement of effective Goldilocks potential due to accumulated complexity. As complexity accumulates, it can create localized environments with even higher potential for further complexity.

While thermodynamic principles suggest complexity will continue to find pathways to increase energy dissipation, they don’t dictate which specific pathways will be taken. For conscious beings like ourselves, the qualitative differences between possible complexity-increasing trajectories matter enormously. The abstract mathematics of G_base and G_cum must ultimately be expressed through the messy, contingent processes of human society and culture.
