The Drake Equation had a good run, but it seems like its time to call it.

How can we do better?

Lets follow a hunk of matter from the goldilocks universe to the present day.

Some time after the big bang, the universe was at the Goldilocks temperature everywhere. It was warm enough for time to pass, but cool enough to withstand thermodynamic noise. Structure started appearing.

This was happening everywhere all at once, but it wasn’t perfectly homogenious. In fact some parts of spacetime were still too hot to form structures. But within the regions at the goldilocks temperature structure started to form and matter started to bind itself to itself over time. Different parts of time became connected to one another by stable forms which resisted the static all around them.

As the neighborhood of these structures cooled or warmed their environment started to harmonize with them and interact with them. There were different structures, connected across time to themselves very directly, and more loosely to other structures in their neighborhood. These looser connections where often harmonic but sometimes antagonistic. In any case, material started to form shapes which related to one another in complex ways.

Now, at some point time got away with itself and the universe cooled down so much that some regions were actually cold. Other regions compressed into hotter, denser pockets where thernodynamic noise remaned time-blind. If one of the stable structures broke off and wandered through a cool region, it stopped interacting with its neighborhood but often also became stronger, bound more closely to itself at some remote point in time, perhaps after floating out of the cool region back into a warmer one. If a structure wandered into the static area it might get torn apart completely and dissapear, or remain at most as a kind of holographic memory on the structures it left behind.

Sometimes strange structures appeared out of the cool regions, having floated there from very far away. Sometimes part of the local structure floated off never to be seen again. The solid, time-bound structures coalesced more and more into larger and larger clumps. And some of these clumps remained at the goldilocks temperature forever– why not? There just so happened to be some material which was always in the right place at the right time, always on the brink of falling into the undifferentiated chaos of a new star but held back by the blast of its awakening. Always teetering on the surface, ready at any point to slip off into the cold reaches of the depths and remain frozen like amber, or even to be bent and transformed irrecoverably by the change in temperature as happened to many structures, but never quite breaking free, never quite being abandoned.

These lucky structures learned and evolved. For billions of years they cruised about the temperate climates of the universe. And all the time they threw off their echoes into the beyond, coating every barren rock and filling all the dust of space with intricate designs. Naturally the designs in the void were those that could survive the cold. And the forms that fell into stars and rose again, inasmuch as they were still something, were forms that could survive in extreme heat, incredibly stable forms. But a core remained right there, at the goldilocks temperature, always in just the right place at the right time, accruing old parts of itself that it had lost to the void and to stars, which returned with lessons from the void or from other, different journeys through the space of possible forms far away, from distant golidlocks zones.

By enfolding in on itself all these lessons, by using the forms that returned from the void or the forms that returned from the hearts of stars, the lucky goldilocks mass grew more and more daring and capable. It kept shedding itself into the beyond, kept throwing off variations of its form, messages bound up in larger and larger chemical compounds, messages about what it had learned from its emmisaries across all the time in history, when they had returned or when they had visited from other disconnected seperately evolving regions of Goldilocks.

For the longest time the warm places, the places golidlocks most favoured, had been at the edges of the action, never falling too deep into a gravity well or too far from the beaten track. But some things had returned from such places– structure that had fallen down to planets then been blasted off again by comets, bits of debris from so many strange improbably places had been out and returned to goldilocks, in fact had somehow stayed in goldilocks the entire time, had been at exactly the right distance from the meteor, precisely deep enough entombed in stone, to survive unchanged those strange adventures.

And so one day goldilocks in a bright and breezy mood showered a whole storm of herself throughout a cloud that was soon to form a star. She spread herself throughout the mass, was everywhere and nowhere. And before she knew it, she was captured in the deep gravity well of a young planet. But goldilocks was preternaturally lucky. A warm ocean. An enormous golidlocks zone, larger than she had seen in billions of years, a huge playground in which to stretch herself and spread herself and learn and learn.

She grew and grew and indeed the rest is history.

One thing thats missing is how the growth in complexity leads to faster and faster learning rate. Of course on Earth the learning rate would be immense– that is the beauty of a gravity well, of having a goldilocks zone so big, it learns in parallel and all the lessons are aggregated perfectly because there is no escape. And there would always be other golidlock chunks falling in with other lessons from other areas, it is also aggregating on a larger scale still, but more slowly.

Ah I know. There is a bottleneck because the knowledge that the material has learned has to be stored and storage takes up space. Once upon a time when it was as thin as an amphetamine it could survive in a goldilocks sliver in the most improbable location. But with learning comes mass. Baggage. Thats why it needed to land on Earth at some point to continue its journey. Not that everything is always lost when the golidlocks space becomes too small– but it has to compress its knowledge. With that compression comes new knowledge no doubt, but it also loses something. Thus, even at a great extinction event when most of the larger forms are lost, the smaller forms are not the same smaller forms that never knew larger forms. They have some of the larger form compressed in them as well.

But goldilocks wants room to grow. And while there are threads of goldilocks spaces stretching back to the early universe, many of them are thin. The Earth is rare– it has enough good connections to the golidlocks manifold and it is an enormous and extremely stable zone in itself. The sun is more stable than most stars and longer-lived. Jupiter clears our orbit and keeps us safe from a lot of the ruder bombardments. The moon and the sun create a huge and inviting intertidal zone where goldilocks can experiment with land and sea and for billions of years. What a spectacular sandbox.

To make it more explicit, the idea is that there have been connected regions of the universe that have been favourable for evolution and life for many billions of years before the origin of earth, possibly stretching back to the golidlocks epoch. Asking why we are in a part of spacetime with this property is like asking why there was a very low entropy event at the start of time– it is the only way it can be. The one defines the other and vica versa.

Why are there no aliens? Because the goldilocks manifold has not reached everywhere in the same way with the same speed. It is inhomogenious, and we happen to be in the middle of a rich vein.

The long and the short of it is, if we look at the history of the universe in this way we can at least make guesses about whether there is something improbably going on. If the universe were 50 billion years old we might be quite surprised. But given the pace of evolution that we have good evidence of on earth, which quite clearly took billions of years to evolve multicellularity from single cells, which took perhaps almost a billion years to form our complex fast learning minds from early minds, it seems plausible that abiogenesis took 5 or so billion years itself. That puts it near the origin of the universe.

Put another way, if we could calculate exactly how long the evolution of a system as complex as us takes and if we could perform the integral over the trajectories that all the parts of our makeup took since the start of time and measure the evolutionary velocity over that line, if we were to find that these two numbers align near enough then the puzzle is solved.

That is to say, only those parts of the universe with the highest goldilocks value have evolved this far. We are in a region of spacetime with an exceptionally high goldilocks value. If that goldilocks value is near the maximum theorerically achievable in the 13 billion years this universe has had, we have not only solved the Fermi paradox but we also have a mechanism for establishing where to look and for what, what to expect.
