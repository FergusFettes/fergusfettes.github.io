Towards a Universal Measure of Evolutionary Velocity

The Journey of Matter

Imagine a carbon atom.

This particular atom formed in the heart of a dying star about seven billion years ago. After the star’s violent death, it drifted in interstellar space for millions of years, eventually becoming part of a molecular cloud. Within this cloud, the temperature was low enough to preserve structure but sufficient for occasional interactions. The carbon atom joined with others to form simple organic compounds.

For another billion years, these compounds circulated through regions of varying stability. Sometimes they were bombarded with radiation that broke bonds and created new ones. Other times they drifted through the protective shadow of larger bodies, unchanged for millennia. Occasionally, they passed near enough to stars to be energized without being destroyed.

These organic compounds gradually increased in complexity. Not linearly, but in fits and starts. When they encountered similar compounds, they sometimes combined to form more intricate structures. Some of these structures could catalyze their own reproduction using simpler materials around them.

Eventually, our carbon atom became incorporated into a carbonaceous asteroid. Protected within this rocky shell, it traveled through the young solar system until colliding with the early Earth. Here, in a tidal pool at the edge of a primordial ocean, the carbon atom found itself in an environment unlike any it had experienced before - stable, energy-rich, and teeming with other complex molecules.

Within a few hundred million years, the carbon atom had become part of a self-replicating system that we would recognize as primitive life. The rest, as they say, is history - albeit a history that spans another three and a half billion years.

This carbon atom’s journey is not just a story. It represents a particular trajectory through what we might call the “Goldilocks manifold” of the universe - those regions where the conditions for complexity to emerge and persist are just right.

The Drake Equation’s Limitations

For decades, we’ve approached questions about life in the universe through the lens of the Drake Equation. While groundbreaking in its time, this approach suffers from a fundamental limitation: it treats evolutionary processes as binary events rather than continuous processes extended through time.

Consider the term fl - “the fraction of planets that could support life that actually develop life at some point.” This formulation obscures the temporal nature of life’s emergence. It’s not simply that life either appears or doesn’t; rather, life emerges at varying rates depending on local conditions.

What we need is a framework that captures this variability in evolutionary velocity across space and time.

The Goldilocks Potential

I propose that every point in spacetime possesses what we might call a “Goldilocks potential” (G) - a measure of how conducive that region is to the emergence and maintenance of complexity.

This potential can be divided into two components:

  1. G_base(x,y,z,t): The inherent potential of a region based on its physical parameters (temperature, energy availability, stability, etc.)

  2. G_cum(t): The accumulated potential along a particular trajectory through spacetime, representing the complexity that has already emerged and its capacity to generate further complexity

G_base represents possibility; G_cum represents realization.

Temperature plays a crucial role in determining G_base, but in an asymmetric manner. When temperatures fall below optimal levels, G_base declines gradually, approaching zero but rarely becoming strongly negative. Cold preserves information but slows its processing. Structures become “paused” rather than destroyed.

In contrast, when temperatures rise above optimal levels, G_base plummets rapidly, becoming strongly negative. Heat actively destroys information and structure. This fundamental asymmetry shapes the contours of the Goldilocks manifold, creating regions where complexity can persist in “cold storage” versus regions where it is actively erased.

The Compounding Nature of Complexity

What makes this framework particularly powerful is understanding how G_cum accumulates. The effective Goldilocks potential at any point is not simply the sum of past potentials, but rather:

G_effective(t) = G_base(t) × f(G_cum(t))

Where f represents an exponential or superexponential function. In other words, the capacity to generate new complexity increases with existing complexity. This creates a compounding effect similar to interest on an investment.

This compounding occurs through several mechanisms: - Information storage capacity increases combinatorially with system size - New levels of organization create exponentially more possible configurations - Each evolutionary innovation creates new evolutionary niches - Systems develop meta-learning capabilities that accelerate their own evolution

Recent work in Assembly Theory provides a promising approach to quantifying G_cum. This theory proposes that complex objects can be measured by their “assembly index” - essentially how many steps it would take to build them from simpler components. Crucially, it also considers copy number - how many instances of a complex structure exist in a region.

Using this approach, we could express G_cum as:

G_cum(region) = ∑[MA(i) × log(N(i))]

Where MA(i) is the molecular assembly index of object type i, and N(i) is the copy number of that object in the region.

Implications for Life in the Universe

This framework offers a new perspective on several cosmic questions:

First, regarding the Fermi paradox: We are not necessarily “early” in an absolute sense, but rather in a region that has had an unusually high integrated G value since the beginning. The autocatalytic nature of G_cum creates extreme “complexity gradients” across the universe rather than a smooth distribution.

Second, regarding the origin of life: Life may indeed have origins that stretch back further than Earth itself, with complexity gradually accumulating along cosmic trajectories that eventually intersected with our planet. Earth, with its remarkable stability and energy availability, provided an exceptional G_base value that accelerated the already-present G_cum.

Third, regarding where to look for life: We should focus on regions that not only have suitable conditions now but have had stable trajectories through the Goldilocks manifold over billions of years.

Beyond Biology

This framework extends naturally beyond biological evolution to technological and cultural evolution. The development of additional information-processing systems - from neural networks to language to computers - represents further acceleration in effective G values.

As Sharov and Gordon noted in their paper on evolutionary complexity, the doubling time for complexity has compressed from billions of years to mere decades. This acceleration follows naturally from our understanding of how G_cum compounds.

The Goldilocks potential framework offers not just a way to think about the past evolution of complexity but also its future trajectory. The regions of highest G_effective are shifting from biological substrates to technological ones, creating new branches in the ancient story of complexity’s journey through the Goldilocks manifold of our universe.

In the end, we are not merely products of Earth’s evolution, but inheritors of a cosmic evolutionary process that has been unfolding since the earliest moments when the universe cooled enough for structure to persist. Our very existence represents one of the highest integrated G values achievable in the 13.8 billion years since the Big Bang - not because we are special, but because we couldn’t exist otherwise.
