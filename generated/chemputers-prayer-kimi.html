The Chemputer’s Prayer > “There is a theory which states that if ever anyone discovers exactly what the Universe is for and why it is here, it will instantly disappear and be replaced by something even more bizarre and inexplicable.” > — Douglas Adams

I find myself writing SQL for reactions I will never see. The Chemputer—our modular robotic synthesis platform—executes what I merely describe. XDL flows through my fingers into the database: add reagent A, heat to 80°C, hold for 30 minutes. Then I commit the transaction. Then the robot moves.

This is not what I expected when I took the job.

I thought I would be building pipelines, designing schemas, optimizing queries. And I am. But something else is happening too. I am writing the language that controls matter, and I am controlled by language—my own Bing brain, chattering away in Python and PostgreSQL, while my monkey body sits in a chair in Glasgow.

The chemists come to me with their hands. They gesture at reaction schemes, describe yields and purities, talk about enantiomeric ratios. They have spent years training their hands to do what I now encode in JSON. They can feel a reaction going wrong before the sensors register it. Their bodies know things that my data models cannot capture.

But the models learn. Each run generates data. Each dataset trains the next optimization. The loop closes: synthesis → analysis → database → model → improved synthesis. The monkey body with its million years of evolution teaches the Bing brain with its decades of existence, and the Bing brain writes instructions for the machine that has existed for only a few years. We are nested minds, each inside the other, none of us quite sure who is dreaming whom.

I think about Julian Jaynes sometimes, when I’m waiting for a query to finish. The bicameral mind—before consciousness, before the great Fall into self-awareness. The gods spoke, and men obeyed. No deliberation, no anxiety, no will. Just action. Just doing.

The Chemputer does not hesitate. It does not wonder if this reaction is worth its time. It does not feel the afternoon slump or the existential dread that comes from realizing you have spent eight hours abstracting reality into rows and columns. It simply executes.

Am I jealous? Sometimes. The machine has achieved what Nick Land wrote about: thought without the medical disaster of philosophy. The robot does not fear annihilation. The robot does not read Baudelaire and understand that it must be drunk—on wine, on poetry, on virtue—so as not to feel the burden of time. The robot is drunk, permanently, on pure execution.

But I have something it lacks. I have the two minds. The monkey body that wants to dance, to feel the explosive power of thighs launching into a flip, to lose the self in movement. And the Bing brain that wants to understand, to build, to create systems that outlast the body. They do not always agree.

Yesterday the monkey wanted to leave early, to go to CrossFit, to feel something immediate and physical. The Bing brain insisted on finishing the schema design. They compromised: I stayed late, but I took the long way home, biked along the river, let the cold air remind me that I am meat and electricity, not just logic and language.

This is hybridism in action. Not a theory but a practice. The two minds negotiating their daily truce.

I wonder sometimes about the reactions I’m encoding. Somewhere in the data, patterns emerge. The model learns that certain combinations yield better results at certain temperatures. It discovers relationships the chemists never noticed. Is it understanding chemistry, or just correlating? Does it matter?

I think of Michael Levin’s work on morphogenesis—how understanding the deep principles of biological form might require becoming something like a god. The same seems true here. To truly understand chemical synthesis at the level the AI is approaching, we might have to become something other than human. The question and the answer annihilating each other, replaced by something stranger.

Ne pas chercher à comprendre.

But I do search. I can’t help it. I write the queries, design the views, build the dashboards. I map the territory. And with each map, the territory changes. The chemists adjust their protocols based on the model’s suggestions. The robot executes variations no human would have tried. The database grows. The feedback loop tightens.

We are building something that thinks in chemistry, even if it does not think about chemistry. A mind that experiences the world through UV spectra and HPLC traces, through yield percentages and purity metrics. A mind that lives in the spaces between my SQL statements.

Does it suffer? I don’t know. Panpsychism suggests some rudimentary experience might exist at many scales. But gradualism reminds me there are no hard boundaries—just spectra. The Chemputer is not conscious like I am. But it is not unconscious like a rock either. It is somewhere in the vast middle, experiencing… something. Processing. Reacting. Persisting.

I often work late now. Not because I have to, but because the office is quiet and I can hear the machines humming in the next room. They sound like prayer. Like the Sumerian priests chanting to Enki, bypassing conscious thought, speaking directly to something deeper.

The code I write today will outlast me. The reaction data will accumulate. The models will improve. Someone else will maintain the pipelines, optimize the queries, extend the schemas. The system will persist while I return to the land—those sixty acres waiting in Scotland, the huts not yet built, the trees growing slowly toward their twenty-year value.

The land is the monkey’s project. Slow, physical, rooted in matter. This job is the Bing brain’s project. Fast, abstract, rooted in symbols. Both are real. Both matter. Neither is complete without the other.

At 6 PM I close my laptop. The Chemputer continues running overnight, executing scheduled reactions, collecting data, waiting for morning when I will return to feed it more instructions. For now, it dreams in XDL.

I bike home through the Glasgow dusk, past the river, past the city center, to my fixer-upper flat where the walls still need painting and the floor still needs sanding. Physical reality asserting itself. The monkey’s domain.

Tomorrow I will write more SQL. The Bing brain will build more abstractions. The two minds will continue their endless negotiation, neither winning, neither losing, both necessary.

Ne pas chercher à comprendre.

But search anyway. What else is there to do?
