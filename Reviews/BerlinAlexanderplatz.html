Berlin Alexanderplatz Review

Review of the book and the film.

Book (Rereading)

Film (Work in Progress)

First Reading

The surface level reading of the film leaves you with a bad taste in your mouth– a man gets out of prison after murdering his wife, is welcomed with open arms by the community of low-grade gangsters and hustlers with whom he associates, eventually gets drawn back into low-grade pimping, robbery etc, then loses his mind.

The initial reading summary would be ‘poor people are scum’, a similar message you would get from Bunuels Viridiana on a totally naieve reading.

Themes

Domestic Violence and Gender

The Fassbinder interpretation focuses almost entirely on Franz et sein filles. One wonders at times if it is really necessary to have 9 hours of women treating him like a prince to exacerbate the awfulness of the violence– but this seems a lot of what is going on.

Fassbinders feminine eye makes it strangely pallatable, as he manages to make his female characters cahracterful and agentic, despite their role as foils for the relationship between Franz and Reinhold.

Evil

‘Why do you let evil into your life?’ or rather, ‘Why was Reinhold1 in that bed?’. Why do you hang around with him at all? Why do you take part in his perverse habits, why do you spend so much time and energy enabling him?

This brings us to our secondary reading– evil begets evil, a sort of cultural determinist take on the motivations of the characters. A murderer gets out of prison, is welcomed back to his community– no wonder he returns to wickedness, when his evil is forgotten or ignored– violence is a valid form of communication. As I reread the book I will see how much this is the case.

The Man

One of the most striking features of the film (per the amount of domestic violence) is the dissonance between the fascination every character has with Franz, and the character himself. He is largely portrayed as a bumbling fool– his motivations are all over the place. His politics, inasmuch as he has them, are the sort of low-grade contrarianism that you see at QAnon rallies (or in more culturally appropriate parlance, he is a bit of a querdenker).

If the second half of the film drags (which it does), it might be a symptom of Fassbinders weakness for his characters, for giving them space and for using what they make of it. The amount of time we spend watching Franz and Mietze kissing and rolling around on the floor seems out of all proportion. In particular, in Ep.XIII, the moment of high drama is rendered almost absurd by the free form, presumably improvised dialogue between Mietze and Reinhold.

But, that brings me to another point– the first murder scene, the scene of Franz beating his wife to death, which is repeated many times. On a purely forensic viewing it would seem to exonerate Franz entirely. He does not beat her particularly badly, hitting her with a wooden spoon at some point, and slapping her a few times. But Fassbinder is not Michael Bay, his film does not live or die on the technical bombasity of the action– by having such a technically tepid main scene, the horror of what it represents is foregrounded.2

On this subject– maybe it suffers a little on the big screen. Maybe it is better as television– I’m thinking particularly of the amount of time Franz’s face is spelled out in five meters of flesh in front of you. Or is that brilliant? The butchery scene in the book is much quieter in the film– maybe this is Fassbinders butcher shop– the face, the plastic face that huge pregnant object.
