    Memories

    Cairo! What a city. My most lasting impression was this: why do so many young Europeans who want to experience the exotic, fly all the way to Mumbai or Delhi, when there is this fabulous exotic beast of a city right on their doorstep? It was shocking how close we were to home– the reason we ended up there was the consistently cheap tickets to Sharm-El-Sheik from anywhere in europe– and yet how far we felt.

    I suppose I have always had a clear association with flying east. The 8 hours you spend crossing continents makes you feel like you are suitably far away. So flying what felt like ‘just across the pond’ and yet being somewhere so radically different– interesting!

    Note on the map, I downloaded my historical data from google maps, but unfortunately it was not as high resolution as I expected. A little disappointing, and I have now started using the OSM tracker, for future travel I will have better traces.

# Convert time

df = df[(df['lat'] > 29) & (df['lat'] < 31) & (df['lng'] > 30) & (df['lng'] < 32)]
df.reset_index(inplace=True)

# Sort data into traces

# Add first row to first trace

    Dar El Salam (دار السلام), ‘Abode of Peace’

    Not to be confused with the Dar es Salaam in Tanzania (or the Muslimgauze album for that matter), this is a district in Cairo.

    The cluster in the south was where we spent the first week. It is something like the highest-density, fastest-growing, youngest population in town. In a phrase, ‘the poor district’. We were asked many times what we were doing there, and told by many people it was dangerous. But, when we met some Expats in a bar in the center, one of them was from Aberdeen– they were all surprised at where we were staying, but when I asked him ‘but, do you really think it is more dangerous than Aberdeen or Glasgow’ he looked offended at the question and said ‘well of course not’.

    Everything is relative, and it is clear that the crime rates in Egypt are very moderate. To us, the district seemed exactly as it was– bustling with youthful exuberance– the transport in the district consisted almost entirely of teenagers in motorized tuktuks with ghetto blasters and LEDs drinking Pepsi, cruising. They were mostly indifferent to our presence– we would ask them to take us somewhere, they would look at one another, laugh, shrug and start driving.

    Eat

    There are three things to eat in Cairo aside from meat: koshary, sandwiches and sweet potatoes.

    Koshary

    Koshary is a dish that is a staple of the Egyptian diet. It is a mix of carbs: rice, lentils, macaroni and chickpeas. But it isn’t all carbs! It’s also performance. Lets say you got the takeaway; once you have your mass of carbs (in a little grease-paper bowl, like a regular noodle box), you are also given the following: one large baggie of tomato sauce, one baggie of spicy sauce, one baggie of garlic sauce, and a baggy of fried onions.

    The fun is in pouring your ingredients one after another onto your base, and then sprinkling the onions on top, and then consuming it in the fastest way possible.

    Sandwiches

    While I do miss the koshary, for me the sandwiches were the real deal. In imagining them, start with a falafel sandwich. This is actually one of the options, and in many was similar (though superior) to what you would find in europe. Now however, I want you to shrink down the size of the piece of bread considerably, and I want you to remove all the contents, and replace it instead with one of the following: mashed potatoes, foul (refried kidney beans), fried aubergines, fried courgette, shakshuka– or one of the other options that I don’t know how to identify [V: Mousaka!]. So you have your tiny sandwhich with a little bit of friend beans inside it– eat it and have another! Order five if you like, they are cheap as chips and designed for fast, efficient, standing snacking– worthy of Mumbai.

    Walk on, don’t worry– you will see another sandwich place! And another!

    Slow cooked sweet potato with spices! Eat it hot, don’t think twice!

    Sleep

    As I was working full time when we were in Cairo, I couldn’t adventure too hard. So we mostly booked AirBnBs or found hotels through Booking. However, Cairo is a fine city for the foot-goer, and we once managed to stumble into a beautiful old, crumbling dame of a hotel– the Nile Zamalek Hotel. We did what you are meant to do in these places, we ordered a room and stayed there for a week, forming a friendly, nodding acquaintance with the concierge and feeling like we were in a classic movie. There is something truly wonderful about finding a place to stay on foot, it will always beat planning, though planning is an occasional necessity.

    Do

    See the pyramids! When I was first in Cairo I refused, or was too stoned and confused and suffering from akathisia or depressed or whatever you are at 20 years old when you haven’t really woken up yet. Well, not this time!

    Victoria wanted to go there and I said, ‘okay, but lets do it properly’. I knew it would be something of a horrorshow, with the skallys and hawkers and hustlers– shafting tourists is practically a national sport in Egypt3– so I thought we should spend at least a day hovering around the district to get the smell of the air. In the end, it was even better– we got a small hotel overlooking the compound and had a whole week basking in the ineffable glory of those monuments. What a place! Things are even stranger than you expect– you understand of course that there are still communities– travellers, performers, poorer districts in Ireland where the horse is still present, but the reality of this in a place like Giza is far odder than you would think.

    • Very regular except– for the young men on horseback.

    It was beautiful to me not just because seeing people on horses doing regular people stuff, hanging out, going about the place, seemed like something that all humans should see– its something that was in our culture for millenia, and its a tragedy that we have lost it. But it was also an amusing and deeply characterful lesson in the force of– what to call it, inequality, situations, contingency. That this young man here, on this motorway divider, should be riding a horse about the place as if it was the most regular thing in the world– was due to the fact that tourists found it appropriate to see the pyramids from horseback, and had the cash to actualise their fantasies– and that this was so present, and so consistent that an entire community had grown around the pyramids, a large community of people who, against all odds, in a megacity populated in parts by teenagers in tuktuks high on pepsi, so lost was it in the stream of technofuturism– that here would be the first time I saw regular folks doing people stuff on horseback. Funny.

    The story of the pyramids itself– a great place really– I will leave for another time.

It was beautiful to me not just because seeing people on horses doing regular people stuff, hanging out, going about the place, seemed like something that all humans should see-- its something that was in our culture for millenia, and its a tragedy that we have lost it. But it was also an amusing and deeply characterful lesson in the force of-- what to call it, inequality, situations, contingency. That this young man here, on this motorway divider, should be riding a horse about the place as if it was the most regular thing in the world-- was due to the fact that tourists found it appropriate to see the pyramids from horseback, and had the cash to actualise their fantasies-- and that this was so present, and so consistent that an entire community had grown around the pyramids, a large community of people who, against all odds, in a megacity populated in parts by teenagers in tuktuks high on pepsi, so lost was it in the stream of technofuturism-- that here would be the first time I saw regular folks doing people stuff on horseback. Funny.

The story of the pyramids itself-- a great place really-- I will leave for another time.
