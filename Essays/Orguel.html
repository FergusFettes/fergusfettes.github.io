Details

Better video showing oligomers etc.

The memory is stored in proteins which bind to the microtubules in each cell, storing the form and history of physiological stresses of that cell.

In order to read off visual information, you need the memory cells of the visual cortex. In order to read somatic information, you need the muscle and nerve cells of the limbs etc.

The structure of the individual proteins encodes information about their location in the body. This happens during a process of activation, in which the local electrochemical signature is imprinted on the protein.

To extract the orguel, a solution of unactivated orguel proteins is injected into the body. These bind to the activated proteins in the cells, forming plaques. The plaques also record in their structure the location of the individual proteins in the cell. Thus the following information is stored: - in the structure of the plaque, the location of the proteins in the cell, and thus the history of the cell - in the elctochemical signature of the proteins, the cell type and species (used to locate the cell in the body)

The orguel is injected alongside a powerful enzyme which breaks down all the other proteins in the body. This is necessary to prevent the orguel from being broken down by the body’s own enzymes, and allows easy extraction of the plaques. The plaques then form longer ribbons which correspond closely to the position in the cell, simplifying the information readout.

Artificial Life Some scientists suggest that the definition-of-life question is a red herring. Life, they say, should be gauged on a continuum, and not granted according to a binary decision. A rock would certainly be low on any continuum of aliveness, and a dog, a tree, and a human being would rank highly. More ambiguous systems would fall in a middleregion of semialiveness—somewhere below bacteria, which almost ev- eryone agrees are alive, and somewhere above rocks. Viruses, which some biologists consider living and others do not, would reside in the upper reaches of this middle ground. Below that would be complexsystems that no one really considers to be alive but that display some behaviors consistent with living organisms—things such as the economy and automobiles. The PolyWorld organisms would fall somewhere be- tween Chevrolets and the flu. There is a particular advantage in regard- ing life in this manner: using systems that no one would classify as truly alive, biologists could nonetheless isolate the qualities of life.

Nick Lane, Life Ascending
