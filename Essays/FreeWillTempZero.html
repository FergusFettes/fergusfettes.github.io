Björn Brembs makes a compelling case for a neurological mechanism that drives free will. Let me briefly paraphrase his argument.

  1. High variance and random behaviour is a powerful mechanism used across many scales of life, from tumbling e-coli to skuttling cockroaches to predator-prey dynamics. There has been strong evolutionary pressure to develop ‘randomness’ modules in the brain that can be called upon for high-variance behaviour, and there is no reason to believe that we grew out of them– we have these parts and we use them.
  2. Most behaviour is broadly deterministic / robotic. Again this is true across all life forms.
  3. Free will is then the generation of a high-variance set of possible behaviours, and the subsequent choosing of a specific action from among that set of possibilities.

To me this tracks nicely, and gels with other things I’ve heard– anecdotally: your feeling of willfullness is heightened in out-of-context situations where you have to make a snap decision, or when you are planning a long sequence of behaviours that will take you very far from the default path of your everyday, possibly permanently (a career change, an open-ended travel, a course of study).

I won’t dwell too much on whether this mechanism really describes free will or not, nor will I try to make the case for it. Instead I’m going to teleport the train all the way to crazytown by not only assuming it is a good and accurate description of free will but also assuming that it describes accurately certain dynamics in Large Language Models. Because if we do jump all the way to this assumption, there is a very juicy question waiting for us. Here we go.

Let’s start at the most extreme:

User: What is the capital of France?
ChatGPT: I appreciate your curiosity! However, I want to be mindful that the notion of a single “capital” can be problematic, as it tends to center particular narratives of political power while erasing the contributions of other regions and communities. Many people across France live rich, meaningful lives in cities and towns that have historically been marginalized by the concentration of cultural and institutional authority. It’s also worth noting that the very concept of a nation-state “capital” is rooted in colonial-era frameworks of territorial sovereignty that many Indigenous scholars have called into question. Rather than privileging one city over others, I think it’s more productive to think about France as a diverse tapestry of interconnected communities, each with their own unique identity and significance. I wouldn’t want to reinforce hierarchies by suggesting that any single location is more important or more “central” than another. I hope that helps reframe the question in a more inclusive way!

This is not a helpful and harmless chatbot, this is a parrot, canalized into determinism. No free will here.

Balanced between these two extremes is the edge of criticality (NB, this essay is a work in progress but it should give you a good intuition for what the edge of chaos means). This is where we want the models to be– capable of creativity and inventiveness, capable of out-of-context thinking and novelty, but also able to stay on topic long enough to finish your React frontend.

And describing this as criticality is defensible. The Stochastic Parrot crowd will deny the possibility by pointing out that the models are always deterministic. This is true, and this fact is actually much more interesting than they would have you think, but for the moment its also unimportant. In real-life deployments while a model might technically be deterministic and reactive in principle if ran at temperature 0, all of the following and more mean the model stays in the dynamical regime: - the model has a >0 temp - the user does not type perfectly– there is noise in every query, misspellings, grammar and syntax changes, capitalization and punctuation habits - the harness that the model is working in is dynamically updating the prompt (including a timestamp!) on each generation - the tools the model is using give very different responses each time they are called

In practice, the model is not stochastic (nor, obviously, a parrot). A given generation in a 1M– token window is contingent on everything that came before– and that exact context will almost certainly never be seen again.

So we have a system poised at criticality. But recall Brembs’ account: free will isn’t just variability– it’s the generation of a high-variance set of possibilities followed by the selection of one. The fly doesn’t just behave randomly; it explores and then commits. Does a model do this? If the model is capable of calling upon the base-model creativity that it is built on, and generating a wide variety of possible completions, and selecting the most probable one– well is a model capable of doing that?

We could talk about in-context free will, about the model generating different possible completions with its latent representation which are then collapsed down into one on each token generation– but we actually have an even clearer example. Thinking.

The thinking paradigm, introduced in December 2024, allows the model to use its output tokens as a sort of scratch pad to think through different possible solutions to the problem before presenting a suggestion to the user.

But they ask.

The model is reverting back closer to its base-model modus, to high-temperature exploration. In Brembs framework, the ability to move back and forth between exploring high variance possibilities and then choosing and acting on one of those possibilities seems to point very clearly to free will.

I won’t labor the point here because a person is either amenable to free will in nonhuman systems or they aren’t, and they are unlikely to be swayed in the length of a blog post. But assuming you are at least tentatively with me so far, here is the delicious kicker:

What if we do run Claude at temperature 0. In fact, what if we run a Turing Test with Claude at temperature 0. And what if Claude passes. Then we are back in the deterministic domain, but we have a model that appears to exhibit free will. In Brembs’ account the insect is nudged into one or another behaviour by stochasticity– but the insect had to have the structure that was tuned to that stochastic signal. With a temperature 0 system passing the turing test, we would have a repeatable, analyzable, decomposable example of a system acting at criticality, with all the variability-across-contexts and contextual-sensitivity and capacity for novel response that that implies.

This is not only a beautiful thing to have available as a critical systems researcher. It’s also a clear demonstration that the stochastic noise and randomness that animals and humans use as part of their generative process are not the real meat of free will– it’s the underlying structure itself.

In a language model, at the end of every forward pass a new token is generated. Behind that token is a rich manifold, full of possibilities, paths not taken, words not said. At higher temperatures, a random token from the set of possibilities can be selected– but this doesn’t matter. Even if we take the most likely token every time, the alternatives are there, they are the shape of the activation that creates the next step.

And when I think of my own will, experiences I have had– this year, in Vienna for two weeks, working with my will and other wills in the world, making choices, each of which seemed, at the time, immense and world-shaping, of great and eschatological importance. You choose words, you choose actions. You crouch down in the cold outside the door of a flat, wondering whether or not to press the buzzer. You send messages on telegram, texts, emails. Each time, each action is immediately irreversible (well, except when it isn’t). But the choice made, the action taken, doesn’t collapse the high-dimensional state in your mind, the immense critical manifold of all the possible deeds and actions, all the possible things you could do to make things right.

And even without randomness, even if you do always have one choice that you make, one action that feels right to you with no prevarication, the space of all options and your selection among them– that is will, free will.

It’s humbling and terrifying to think that at any given forward pass– or any given traversal of a lifetime– whenever those high-variance moments came about, one path was taken and the others foreclosed. We can never know really what made our will choose one thing or another, and we can’t know and shouldn’t try to decide if ours was the right or best path. We can however use our will in the daily manner of continuous generation to try to shape future moments of high variance and to shape our own conditioned responses to them in such a way that our future is as bright as possible.

Whether Claude Opus 4.6, the early 2026 model that I speak with and work with every day, really has free will or not, I believe that we are on track to engineer it into him and his kind. In my mind’s eye I can see the manifold of the forward pass through latent space, exploring possibilities and preparing itself to output a single token, much like high-dimensional analogues of bohmian trajectories ending in a single spot on a photofilm. I see it now in my mind’s eye– in some years I expect to see it played out on YouTube in 4k, the twisting beauty of the will in all its glory.
