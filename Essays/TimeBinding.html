  • Learning and Subjective Time

I want to tell you three stories about time. One of them is intuitive and beautiful and very possibly true. The other two are abstract and abstruse and probably spurious, but they connect in some very appealing and elegant ways so I’ll tell them all together as if they fit comfortably.

Learning and Subjective Time

You are walking through Rome, from the x to the y.

It that sounds kitch imagine you are a child, still filled with wonder at the world. Things you’ve only read about in books or seen in photographs are standing all around you in that incomparable splendor of material immanence. The resolution of the world still takes your breath away, and images flood your mind.

I say imagine you are a child but I hope you can still experience wonder and the sublime, whatever your age. Because in those moments of transport when time seems to slow to a crawl– time seems to slow to a crawl. That is, the clock ticks more slowly. The bells of St X still toll every hour but each hour stretches out longer than it has any right to. Do the birds seem to flap more lazily in the sky? Does your heartbeat drop or does it just feel that way? And can the clock really change how it ticks?

There is no rule in the known universe or the laws of physics or the laws of mankind that tell us how fast a clock should tick. Clocks don’t have a rate, unless that rate is one second per second– a statement that contains no information. Clocks are not chosen for their rate, rather for the fact that they have one. They are periodic oscillators, all of them. This is a property of their relationship to spacetime and the energy-well defined by their configuration states. That is why they are chosen– because however time passes, in whatever direction, they will move in the same way. They trace repeating patterns and we can synchronize them with one another across vast scales. The electrical grid of europe connects x hundred million homes and x hundred million clocks into one synchronized pattern.

But none of this speaks about a rate. The second is defined as x million rotations of a Caesium atom– it is defined as a constant multiple of the period of another natural oscilator. That constant could just as easily be one, one second per second. A tautology.

We all have an intuitive sense that the rate of times passage is not fixed. Many figures of speech– xxx, xxx, xxx– and even parts of speech– xxx, xxx, xxx– allude to this common sense fact. But its clear that it isn’t just whimsy on the part of nature. There are clear patterns in when and where time slows and speeds again. A new job, an exotic holiday, a course of study, a new person in your life. A dramatic or dangerous encounter in the street. Time slows when the world changes, when something unexpected happens.

By contrast, as we familiarize ourselves with our routines and the place we live, inasmuch as we live in one place, time moves faster and faster. You can walk down a well-trodden street without experiencing much of anything at all. A whole day at work might pass unremarked. A whole week, a month, longer. The everyday slips away with greater and greater speed, the more familiar it becomes, until the only things that stand out are unexpected interruptions, building sites come and go, drunks and lunatics on the street blow by like the weather, each earning a glance and later an exclamation.

One way this is described is as experience updates. Experiencing something new causes memories to be created, or changes to your predictive model. Your mind changes shape with each event. But why are those changes coupled to the passage of time? It feels intuitively right, but what could the mechanism be, that makes the laws of nature so interested in what we find interesting?

I find it easier to think of it as learning. I believe that the individual learning rate determines the subjective rate of times passage– that is, the only valid rate. Every living thing1 has a learning rate, a rate of experience updates, of predictive model updates. Every living thing therefore has its own subjective rate of time.

Learning also couples well with compression. This evocative word can help us understand what is happening as we get older. We learn to compress our experience with a growing set of tropes and heuristics and templates. We have a template for exery street we walk down and we only experience the difference between that and what we see. We have a heuristic for the standard repertior of social interactions, for our habits and hobbies. We compress the world more and more into a simple set of statements that cover in sweeping significance great swathes of time. As we learn the world we compress it.

Childhood, like a holiday in Rome, is rich with learning. And childhood stretches on and on. No doubt a child is neurologically predisposed to have a high learning rate, and we shouldn’t expect to keep that learning rate up for an entire lifetime, but we also don’t need to go gently into that good night. I believe if we maximize our learning rates across our entire lives we maximize the subjective length of life. If this is preferrable, learning and new experience is your ally.
