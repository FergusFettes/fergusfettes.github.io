And because humanity is just this magnificent natural agency by which the past lives in the present and the present for the future, I define HUMANITY, in the universal tongue of mathematics and mechanics, to be the TIME-BINDING CLASS OF LIFE.
Alfred Korzybski, Manhood of Humanity, 1921

In Mind Contra Body I left our two actors, the body and the mind, estranged. In the weeks since I wrote it, I have thought more and more about the nature of time and the way we embed it in the world around us. I mentioned Korzybski and time-binding in passing in that essay, but that theme has stuck with me and grown to more and more dominate my thoughts. I want to unpack and expand Korzybski’s thesis. After all, we have learned a great deal about ourselves and our biology in the last hundred years, and our relationship to time has evolved considerably.

Let us deflate Korzybski’s claim a little. Humanity is not the only time-binding class of life. Animals have been binding time since they came on the scene– the genome is a lossy compression of all the choices made by your ancestors, a summary of all they experienced. Animal and human minds bind time into memory. Humankind invests more effort than most in binding their minds to the material world with art and technology, and bind time more deliberately and intensely. And now information technology binds it tighter and interacts with the artifacts of our ancestors in novel and dangerous ways.

I think what has happened, and what characterizes humankind and our relationship to time, is a great shift in its structure and its shape as we percieve it and interact with it. Time and history and our ancestors are so present, and we are in such constant discourse with them, and time wraps itself around us so thickly that we see the world through a glass darkly if we see it at all. Time seems almost to smother us sometimes, like we are drowning in the stuff or being crushed alive.

And I no longer know how fanciful this description is, how metaphorical, and how literal. As I write these words my fingers play across an array of symbols, greek letters, arabic numerals, the sun and moon, the world. I use these symbols to manipulate a machine made through the collaboration of academics and engineers from dozens of countrys, from all the rich and educated parts of the world. It is made from materials mined by and from the poorer parts, assembled in another. It is controlled by a mathematical, logical, symbolical system of systems that has been assembled over the course of a century by hundreds of thousands of people trained in this incomprehensible craft, a craft of which I am a practitioner, a craft that is like writing but writing in the language of machines in a way that invokes machines that moves machines that spreads like a ghost from machine to machine.

Trying to really understand this one thing is so far beyond my capacities, so far beyond what my primate brain can hold in awareness at any one instant. And this machine that I write on may be the most incomprehensible thing in my home, the densest accumulation of time and effort and human will and labor and ingenuity, but there is so much else. Every device, digital or mechanical. Every book. Even something as simple as the cloth against my skin has had a journey across the globe and through the designs of innumerable craftspersons across time in so many permutations. The paint on my walls is the product of centuries of iteration. The walls themselves, echoes of a lineage of architects and builders that goes back to Babylon and beyond.

So forgive me my mysticism. The world is mind-boggling. If you will, allow me to crack you open, fall with me:

Then Space - began to toll,

As all the Heavens were a Bell,
And Being, but an Ear,
And I, and Silence, some strange Race,
Wrecked, solitary, here -

And then a Plank in Reason, broke,
And I dropped down, and down -
And hit a World, at every plunge,
And Finished knowing - then -

Level 1: The Thread

What does it mean to bind time? At its root, it means that the state of a system at one moment can constrain, inform, or determine the state of that system at a later moment through some durable medium. The genome is the most ancient such medium. It is like a molecular tape on which the past has been inscribed, a lossy compression of ancestral environments cached in chemistry. At least in the writing– each ancestor all the way back to the Archean had its chance and took it and contributed its part. The past was linear and it is written onto a chemical cache that is also one-dimensional– but this resemblance is superficial. The compression is holistic, and the sequence in which it accumulated survives nowhere in the way it is used. Nor does the body read it as a head reads a tape, one locus after the next. The body is a thousand read-heads at once, each probing the same store in a different dialect, each drawing out of it a different system — the eye and the liver and the immune cell all transcribing the one archive simultaneously, building from it a thousand incommensurable answers.

Thus thread of time is tangled into a hairball. All of it is present, everywhere, all at once– or none of it, as the organism that evolves from ontogenesis is always an original interpolation in the morphospace the genome defines. Perhaps I was being too generous to the genome when I said it binds time, perhaps it simply accumulates it into an abstract possibility space built up out of the mutation and selection of proteins over the immensity of history.

But then comes the mind. At some point that ball of potential starts to breed neurons which start to bind the world and the self together. Michael Levin suggests that living things are things with a cognitive lightcone larger than their own bodies. That means that they integrate part of their environment into their self-image, their state, their being-in-the-world.
