The ‘Tree’ of Life

The most surprising thing I learned when studying the origin of life was about the structure of the root of the tree.

The structure of the network of evolitionary relatedness is usually imagined– and described– as ‘the tree of life’. A literal tree.

However, it looks like we actually came from an incredibly strange and diverse place, and there was no Last Universal Common Ancestor (LUCA) as is commonly understood.

The Evolution of Culture

… obviously I need some evidence for this but …

I think often about Babylon, and the Tower. For me, this story has always been a very specific metaphor1– about the end of the universal common language, the break between myself and others, the dividing up of humankind2. Of course I always thought that this myth referred to a mythical prehistory in which people truly understood one another, and essentially comes down to anxiety about solipsistic alienation. But I never quite appreciated the image it gives of the world post-Babylon– and, for the record, I think we must assume that we are all and ‘have always been’ post-Babylon.

quote from the bible? or snow crash? or julian jaynes?

But the message that I am interested in here is not probing pre-Babylon, but post-Babylon. The post-Babylon era, the Era of Babel, the era of Diversity is what is interesting.

I think, those of us who are very online, and have sauntered and lurked through LW, EA, 4CH, Juggaloes, MLP:FIM, Reddit, Instagram, Twitter, the original SilkRoad, TOR, Twitch, DeviantArt, Rotten.com, HN, Tumblr etc. et.c can have some inkling of the diversity of ideas and opinions that can exist. But I don’t think most of us appreciate how diverse and strange the era 10,000 - 3,000 years ago was. I think the number of incredibly beliefs and lenses on the world would boggle the mind.

To provide some evidence of this, I would like to proposed the following reading list (which I confess I haven’t read in its entirety myself):

  • Richard Burtons Anatomy of Melancholy
  • Edmund Spencers The Faerie Queane
  • Thomas Browns Urn Burial
  • Johnathan Swifts ‘…’

… (instead of this, go through these and find a list of strange ideas as examples) …

These works provide a fairly modern glimpse of the range of indescribably strange ideas that still existed in society in only the last 500 years. Now read this essay on Homer, and try to extrapolate back to the pre-Homeric period, where there were entire ethnicities devoted only to endlessly freestyling epic poetry. And try to imagine the number of things that people might have believed.

The thesis here is that the past is far, far stranger than we give it credit for– and similar to the tree of life, we are not experiencing a branching tree of culture evolving into new forms, but the paring back of a diverse range of impossible ideas into a smaller and smaller group of concrete cultures.

Or maybe it isn’t so simple– that once eg. English has dominated every other language, it becomes large enough to sustain smaller languages within it. That the rhizome continues.

The Evolution of Language

Similarly, language is often seen as descending in a tree-like fashion from proto-indo-european. But I suggest that the early languages were as strange and diverse within themselves as the early anscestors of all life.
