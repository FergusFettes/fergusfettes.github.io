Computational History Research Summary

Biological Anchors Report

Ajeya Cotra’s “Forecasting Transformative AI with Biological Anchors” (2020) provides the foundational work on computational requirements for AI development. The report was developed between May 2019 and July 2020, spanning the GPT-2 to GPT-3 era.

Key estimates from the evolutionary anchor: - Total computation by all animal brains throughout evolution: ~1e41 FLOP - Timeframe: ~1e16 seconds (from first animals with neurons to humans) - Population estimate: ~1e20 animals at any given time - Average computation per animal: ~1e5 FLOP/s

The calculation assumes that simulating evolution would require reproducing all neural computation throughout history. However, this has been critiqued - some argue you’d also need to simulate environmental stimuli and contexts, not just isolated neural firing.

More recent analysis has refined these estimates. The thermodynamic approach using Landauer’s principle gives an upper bound of 2e40 FLOP, while a caloric approach considering brain energy consumption provides 6e40 FLOP as the upper bound.

Distribution of computation across species is surprisingly flat - nematodes contribute ~1e25 FLOP/s while humans contribute ~7e24 FLOP/s to the total.

Seth Lloyd’s Computational Universe

Seth Lloyd’s 2001 Physical Review Letters paper “Computational capacity of the universe” provides fundamental bounds on computation. His key findings:

  • The visible universe has computed ~1e122 operations on ~1e92 bits
  • The “ultimate laptop” (1kg mass, 1L volume) could perform 1e51 operations/second on 1e32 bits
  • These represent both upper bounds for possible computers and lower bounds for simulating the universe

Lloyd’s work builds on results from Hans Joachim Bremermann, Norman Margolus, and Lev Levitin regarding fundamental computational limits.

The Margolus-Levitin theorem sets maximum computational speed at 6 × 1e33 operations per second per joule - another fundamental physical constraint.

Landauer’s Principle and Thermodynamic Limits

Landauer’s principle establishes the minimum energy cost for irreversible computation: kT ln(2) per bit operation, where k is Boltzmann’s constant and T is temperature.

At room temperature (300K): 2.87 × 1e-21 J per operation

This has been experimentally verified using single colloidal particles in modulated double-well potentials, confirming the theoretical bound holds in practice.

Current computers (as of 2012) use approximately 1e9 times more energy than the Landauer limit, suggesting significant room for efficiency improvements.

Solar Energy Budget Analysis

Earth receives 173,000 TW of solar energy continuously (1.73 × 1e17 W). The solar constant is measured at 1.361 kW/m² at solar minimum.

However, the sun’s luminosity has increased over geological time. The “faint young sun paradox” indicates solar output was ~70% of current levels 4.5 billion years ago. Taking this evolution into account, average solar power over Earth’s history was approximately 85% of current levels.

Total energy received over 4.5 billion years: ~2.1 × 1e34 J

Converting via Landauer’s principle: ~7 × 1e54 potential operations

This means biological evolution used only ~1e-13 of Earth’s total available energy budget for computation.

Current Global Computing Capacity

Multiple sources estimate current global computing capacity: - 2015 estimate: 2 × 1e20 to 1.5 × 1e21 FLOP/s - Top 500 supercomputers (2021): 3 × 1e18 FLOP/s combined - Projected growth: 2.25x per year through 2027

Timeline Implications

At current exponential growth rates (doubling every ~2 years), artificial computation will match the evolutionary anchor of 1e41 FLOP within approximately 84 years.

This supports the thesis that historical progress should be measured in computational throughput rather than calendar time. The next century may contain more “computational history” than the entire span of biological evolution.

The gap between current efficiency and Landauer limits suggests this transition could happen even faster with better energy efficiency, though fundamental physics will eventually constrain further improvements.

Additional Context

The biological anchors work has influenced AI governance and safety research significantly. Original timeline estimates from the report suggested ~15% probability of transformative AI by 2036, with median estimates around 2050.

Recent compute forecasts expect AI-relevant compute to grow from 10M H100-equivalents today to 100M by 2027, representing continued exponential scaling in line with these projections.
